# Parenting Milestones — Month by Month (0-36 months)

Source: WHO, AAP, CDC developmental milestones (updated 2022)

## 0-3 Months

### What to expect
- **Motor**: Lifts head briefly, grasps finger, startles at loud sounds
- **Social**: Social smile (6-8 weeks), tracks faces, coos
- **Sleep**: 14-17 hours, no pattern yet. Survival mode for parents.
- **Feeding**: Every 2-3 hours (breast or bottle)

### Parent tip
This phase is about survival, not optimization. Lower all expectations for productivity.
Accept help. Sleep when the baby sleeps (cliché because it's true).

## 3-6 Months

### What to expect
- **Motor**: Rolls over, reaches for objects, puts everything in mouth
- **Social**: Laughs, recognizes parents, babbles (ba-ba, ma-ma sounds)
- **Sleep**: 12-16 hours, starting to consolidate night sleep
- **Feeding**: May start solids at 6 months (pediatrician guidance)

### Parent tip
Gentle routines start working now. Consistent bedtime (bath → feed → book → sleep) pays off.

## 6-9 Months

### What to expect
- **Motor**: Sits independently, starts crawling, pincer grasp developing
- **Social**: Stranger anxiety begins, understands "no", waves bye-bye
- **Sleep**: 12-15 hours, 2 naps. Sleep training window opens.
- **Feeding**: Solid foods 2-3x/day alongside milk

### Parent tip
Baby-proof EVERYTHING. They're mobile now. Sleep training (if you choose to) works best at 6-7 months.

## 9-12 Months

### What to expect
- **Motor**: Pulls to stand, cruises furniture, may take first steps
- **Social**: Points at things, says first real words, separation anxiety peaks
- **Sleep**: 12-14 hours, 2 naps (transitioning to 1 nap by 12-15 months)
- **Feeding**: 3 meals + snacks, self-feeding attempts (messy but important)

### Parent tip
Separation anxiety is NORMAL and healthy. It means attachment is secure. Consistent goodbye
ritual helps: "I'll be back after your nap. I love you. Bye-bye!" — same words every time.

## 12-18 Months

### What to expect
- **Motor**: Walking (12-15 months), climbing, stacking 2-3 blocks
- **Language**: 5-20 words, understands far more than says, points to communicate
- **Social**: Parallel play (near other kids, not with them), imitates adults
- **Tantrums**: Starting. Small frustrations feel enormous to them.

### Parent tip
Name their emotions: "You're frustrated because you can't reach it." This builds emotional
vocabulary that pays dividends for years.

## 18-24 Months

### What to expect
- **Motor**: Running, climbing stairs with help, kicking ball
- **Language**: 50+ words, 2-word phrases ("more milk", "daddy go")
- **Social**: Says "NO" constantly (healthy independence), tantrums intensify
- **Behavior**: Testing boundaries is their full-time job

### Parent tip
Tantrums at this age are not manipulation — they're a developing brain overwhelmed by big
emotions with zero coping skills. Stay calm, validate, wait it out. "I see you're angry. I'm
right here." Consistency + calm = eventual self-regulation.

## 24-36 Months

### What to expect
- **Motor**: Jumping, pedaling trike, drawing circles, using scissors (with help)
- **Language**: Full sentences, "why?" constantly, imaginative storytelling
- **Social**: Pretend play, early sharing, seeks approval, developing empathy
- **Toilet**: Readiness signs appear (many kids ready between 24-36 months)

### Parent tip
The "why?" phase is exhausting but critical — it's how they build mental models of the world.
When you can't answer anymore: "That's a great question. What do YOU think?" Works surprisingly well.

---

## Red Flags (consult pediatrician)

| Age | Concern |
|-----|---------|
| 3 months | No social smile, no eye contact |
| 6 months | Not reaching for objects, not babbling |
| 12 months | Not pointing, no words, not standing with support |
| 16 months | No words at all |
| 24 months | Fewer than 50 words, no 2-word phrases |
| Any age | Loss of previously acquired skills |

**When in doubt, ask.** Early intervention (before age 3) has the highest success rate for
developmental concerns. Better to check and be reassured than to wait and miss a window.
