# Arabic Dialects — Deep Dive Reference

## Egyptian Sub-Dialects

### Cairo (القاهرة)
- Standard Egyptian dialect — what TV/film uses
- "إيه ده" (what is this), "كده" (like this), "بتاع" (thing/belonging)
- Pronunciation: ج = hard G (جمال = Gamal)
- Most widely understood Arabic dialect globally
- Basis for Egyptian media Arabic

### Alexandria (اسكندراني)
- Faster pace, Mediterranean influence
- "يا سيدي" used more frequently
- Slight Italian/Greek loanwords from history
- More cosmopolitan vocabulary

### Upper Egyptian (صعيدي)
- Conservative, distinct vocabulary
- "ج" pronounced as "G" (like Cairo) or "J" (in some areas)
- Unique expressions: "يا عم" (more frequent than Cairo)
- Important: Never mock Saidi dialect in content — deeply offensive
- Represents ~30M people

### Delta (فلاحين)
- Rural Egyptian
- Distinct pronunciation patterns
- "ق" sometimes pronounced as hamza
- Targeted for agricultural/rural products

## Saudi Sub-Dialects (Extended)

### Najdi (Central — Riyadh, Qassim, Ha'il)
- **The prestige dialect** — Saudi national media uses this
- "وش" for what, "حق" for belonging
- Conservative pronunciation
- "كذا" (like this), "ذيك" (that — feminine)
- Future: "ب" prefix → "بسوي" (I will do)
- Unique: "زين" (good/OK), "وايد" used less than Gulf coast

### Hejazi (Western — Jeddah, Mecca, Medina)
- More relaxed, Egyptian-influenced (historical trade routes)
- "كدا" instead of "كذا"
- More open vowels
- "داحين" / "دحين" = now (unique to Hejaz)
- More cosmopolitan vocabulary (Hajj/Umrah tourism influence)
- Subvariant: Medina dialect slightly different from Jeddah

### Eastern Province (الشرقية — Dammam, Khobar, Dhahran)
- Close to Bahraini dialect
- Oil industry English loanwords
- "شلونك" (how are you — shared with Kuwaiti)
- More Gulf-coast features than Najdi

### Southern Saudi (جنوبي — Abha, Jizan, Najran)
- Most distinct Saudi subdialect
- Different pronunciation of several consonants
- Unique vocabulary not found elsewhere
- Closer to Yemeni in some features
- **Important for marketers:** target with MSA, not Najdi

### Northern Saudi (شمالي — Tabuk, Ha'il borders)
- Jordanian/Bedouin influence
- "شو" sometimes used (Levantine influence)
- Distinct Bedouin vocabulary

## Gulf Micro-Dialects

### Kuwaiti (كويتي)
- "شلونك" (how are you)
- "زين" (good)
- Persian loanwords: "جاي" (tea), "دريشة" (window)
- Indian food terms (large Indian community influence)
- Notably more Persian than other Gulf

### Bahraini (بحريني)
- Two variants: Sunni Bahraini vs Baharna (Shia Bahraini)
- Baharna: Persian-heavy, distinct intonation
- Sunni: closer to Saudi Eastern Province
- Small market — usually targeted with Gulf-neutral

### Emirati (إماراتي)
- "هلا" (hello — very common)
- "شحالك" (how are you)
- "يالس" (sitting — used where others say "قاعد")
- More English loanwords than other Gulf (Dubai's internationalism)
- Subvariant: Abu Dhabi vs Dubai (minor differences)

### Qatari (قطري)
- Very close to Gulf standard
- Small population → usually targeted with Gulf-neutral
- High English proficiency → code-switching common

### Omani (عماني)
- Most distinct Gulf dialect
- Different from Gulf mainstream
- "شحوالك" (how are you — unique form)
- Some features closer to Yemeni
- Ibadi Islamic tradition influences terminology

## Levantine Sub-Dialects

### Lebanese (لبناني)
- **Most French-influenced Arabic:**
  - "بونجور" (bonjour), "ميرسي" (merci), "Ca va?"
  - "بارفيه" (parfait/perfect)
  - "أوتيل" (hotel from French hôtel)
- Prestigious in pop culture (music, fashion)
- "كيفك" (how are you)
- "هيدا" (this — masculine), "هيدي" (this — feminine)
- Softer intonation than other Arabic

### Syrian (سوري)
- Close to Lebanese but less French
- "شلونك" / "كيفك" both used
- Pre-war: sophisticated, literary reputation
- Diaspora has spread Syrian dialect globally
- "هلق" (now), "لسا" (still)

### Jordanian (أردني)
- Bedouin and Palestinian influences
- "إيش" (what), "هسا" (now)
- More conservative than Lebanese
- "عادي" (normal — used constantly)
- Subvariant: East Bank (Bedouin) vs Palestinian-Jordanian

### Palestinian (فلسطيني)
- Close to Jordanian but distinct
- "شو" (what), "هلا" (now)
- "كيف حالك" (more formal form common)
- Village dialects differ from city (Nablus ≠ Ramallah ≠ Gaza)
- Gaza: more Egyptian influence
- West Bank: more Jordanian influence

## Iraqi Sub-Dialects

### Baghdadi (بغدادي)
- "شنو" (what), "شكو ماكو" (what's up — iconic phrase)
- "چ" sound (ch) — unique to Iraqi: "چلب" (dog), "چان" (was)
- "گ" sound (g) — "گال" (said)
- Persian and Turkish loanwords
- Most recognizable Iraqi dialect

### Southern Iraqi (جنوبي — Basra)
- "حجي" (talk/man) — very southern
- Closer to Gulf dialects
- Distinct marsh Arab vocabulary (Ma'dan)
- Oil industry English terms

### Kurdish-influenced Northern (موصلي)
- Kurdish loanwords
- Different intonation
- "أكو" / "ماكو" (there is / there isn't)
- Less commonly targeted in marketing

## Maghrebi Deep Dive

### Moroccan Darija (دارجة مغربية)
- **Most distinct from standard Arabic:**
  - "فين" (where), "كيداير" (how are you)
  - "بزاف" (a lot — from French beaucoup)
  - "نورمال" (normal), "ترانكيل" (calm — French tranquille)
  - "ماشي" (not / it's fine — context-dependent)
  - Berber substrate: "أزول" (hello in Amazigh)
- Heavy vowel reduction (Casablanca → "كازا")
- **Content strategy:** Always specify Moroccan Arabic, never assume Gulf audience will understand

### Algerian (دزيرية)
- French even heavier than Moroccan
- "واش" (what/is it?), "كيفاش" (how)
- "حاجة" (thing — also Egyptian)
- "بصح" (but/however — unique to Algerian)
- Numbers sometimes in French
- Code-switching with French is NORMAL — not laziness

### Tunisian (تونسي)
- French + Italian influence
- "آش" / "شنوة" (what)
- Most European-influenced Maghrebi dialect
- Smaller market — usually combined with Maghrebi strategy

### Libyan (ليبي)
- Between Maghrebi and Egyptian
- "ها" question particle: "واش" / "ها"
- Italian loanwords (colonial history)
- "دوكا" (now)
- Eastern Libya (Benghazi) closer to Egyptian
- Western Libya (Tripoli) closer to Tunisian

## Sudanese (سوداني)

- Distinct from all other Arabic
- "كيفك" / "إنت زاتو" (you yourself)
- "دا" / "دي" for this (masculine/feminine)
- Nubian vocabulary in northern Sudan
- "يا زول" (man/dude — iconic Sudanese)
- British English loanwords (colonial history)
- Slow, deliberate pace — sounds calm

## Yemeni (يمني)

- Archaic features preserved from old Arabic
- "كيف حالك" standard form used
- Distinct intonation (musical quality)
- "وين" (where) — shared with Levantine
- Northern Yemeni ≠ Southern Yemeni (Aden more cosmopolitan)
- Very conservative content requirements
- Smallest digital audience among Arab countries (internet penetration low)

## Pronunciation Guide for Audio/Video Content

### Letters that differ by dialect

| Letter | MSA | Gulf | Egyptian | Levantine | Iraqi | Moroccan |
|--------|-----|------|----------|-----------|-------|----------|
| ق (qaf) | /q/ | /g/ or /q/ | glottal stop /ʔ/ | glottal stop /ʔ/ | /g/ | /q/ |
| ج (jim) | /dʒ/ | /j/ (y sound) | /g/ (hard g) | /ʒ/ (like French j) | /dʒ/ or /tʃ/ | /ʒ/ |
| ك (kaf) | /k/ | /k/ (sometimes /tʃ/) | /k/ | /k/ | /k/ or /tʃ/ (چ) | /k/ |
| ث (tha) | /θ/ (th) | /θ/ | /s/ | /t/ or /s/ | /θ/ | /t/ |
| ذ (dhal) | /ð/ | /ð/ | /z/ or /d/ | /z/ or /d/ | /ð/ | /d/ |
| ظ (dha) | /ðˤ/ | /ðˤ/ | /zˤ/ | /zˤ/ or /dˤ/ | /ðˤ/ | /dˤ/ |

This matters for: voice-over scripts, podcast content, video subtitles accuracy.
